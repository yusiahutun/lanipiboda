How PETA has changed the fashion industry over the last 40 years and what's next

February 2024 · 5 minute read

This year marks the 40th anniversary of PETA US, the largest animal rights organisation in the world. Over the decades, PETA affiliates have been an unstoppable force in transforming the fashion industry into a kinder place. All those years ago, it was hard to imagine that fur would ever go out of style – but today, Gucci, Chanel, Vivienne Westwood, and Calvin Klein are just a few of the many brands that have declared that fur is over and will no longer use it in their collections. The Queen has renounced it. And there is talk of a fur sales ban in the UK. So, how did we get here? Through hard-hitting eyewitness investigations, catwalk disruptions, colourful protests, celebrity support, and a whole lot of nudity.

Once, people could claim ignorance of the cruelty in the fur trade. But thanks to industry exposés by PETA and our international affiliates, fashion insiders can no longer hide behind excuses – it's common knowledge that pelts come from animals who suffered on filthy, severely crowded factory farms or were caught in steel-jaw traps and bludgeoned to death. And while we'd have loved to hold news conferences about such investigations and then sit back and wait for the media to cover them, we found that it was rarely that easy. We had to be bold to make people sit up and take notice. So PETA US took over Calvin Klein's office, and as a result, in 1994, the label was the first high-end designer to go fur-free. We also stormed Jean Paul Gaultier's Paris store, and the brand eventually ditched fur, too.

Fashion insiders can no longer hide behind excuses

In February, PETA closed the book on its groundbreaking "I'd Rather Go Naked Than Wear Fur" campaign after a smashing three-decade run. The campaign started with The Go-Go's – the rock icons bared all in 1990 – and attracted superstars such as Christy Turlington, Eva Mendes, Gillian Anderson, and dozens of other celebrities who were happy to drop their clothes to expose the cruelty inherent in fur. Their ads appeared everywhere, including New York's Times Square, where mammoth billboards featuring Pamela Anderson, Taraji P Henson, and P!nk were unveiled during Fashion Weeks.

But we didn't stop at fur. No one in the fashion industry was talking about angora until a PETA Asia investigation showed rabbits on farms in China tied down and screaming as their fur was torn out by the fistful. Stunned by the cruelty, retailers met with PETA and our international affiliates, which persuaded over 300 brands, including Zara, Marks & Spencer, Tommy Hilfiger, and Ralph Lauren, to remove angora from their ranges.

Mohair, too, is on its last legs. PETA uncovered rampant abuse on 12 goat farms in South Africa, where most of the world's mohair is produced: goats were dragged by the horns, thrown across the floor, and mutilated. Some were still conscious when their throats were cut. It wasn't long after PETA's exposé that H&M, Inditex, the Arcadia Group (which owns Topshop), and other fashion giants dropped it.

Animals aren't commodities to be made into wool coats or leather boots... They are sentient individuals

PETA's campaign against the exotic-skins industry – a house of horrors for alligators, ostriches, snakes, lizards, and other animals – is making waves, too. Chanel, Diane von Furstenberg, Victoria Beckham, and many others have made the socially responsible decision to leave exotic skins out of their designs.

Now, we are going after wool and leather.

The landmark 2014 PETA US investigation of shearing sheds in the US and Australia showed sheep being punched and kicked, beaten with a hammer, and slammed into the floor. Six shearers in Australia were booked on dozens of counts of cruelty to animals, the first charges ever filed against wool workers for abusing sheep. In February, following a 13thwool-industry investigation, another Australian shearer pleaded guilty to cruelty – and after a PETA complaint led to criminal charges, so did a Scottish farmer who had been filmed punching sheep in the face.

Our exposé of the leather trade in Bangladesh revealed that the industry is a living hell not only for animals but also for humans and the environment – and PETA supporters have taken that message to London, Milan, and Paris fashion weeks over the last few seasons by pouring buckets of "toxic slime" over their heads to symbolise the toxicity of leather production.

Animals aren't commodities to be made into wool coats or leather boots. They are sentient individuals, and the more we learn about them and how fascinating they are, the less of an excuse we have for wearing them. Sheep can recognise 50 faces for up to two years, using the same neural pathways as humans. Cows communicate with each other via subtle facial movements. And minks make a purring sound when they're happy, just like our cats at home.

PETA founder Ingrid Newkirk

Today's compassionate consumers want humane, sustainable fashion: a survey by Bain & Co found that animal welfare is a key factor for the high-spending millennial demographic. And businesses are responding: material innovation company Modern Meadow is developing laboratory-grown leather, and a group of forward-thinking university students from Colombia have created a vegan wool from coconut and hemp, to cite just a couple of examples from among many.

PETA is leading the way. In the past few years, we've launched a vegan leather clutch in collaboration with The Kooples, an anti-fur pin with tattoo artist Grace Neutral, and a Beyond Skin shoe design that has been worn by Natalie Portman. We've celebrated innovation with our Fashion Awards, applauded pioneering vegan menswear, and inspired the fashion designers of the future to make compassionate choices.

And we aren't about to stop there. Welcome to the future – it's vegan.

Ingrid Newkirk is Founder of People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals (PETA)
